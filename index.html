Queer Representation in Media

And Why It Matters

Portfolio

About

Oftentimes, education on minority identities for those outside of that minority community is done through media representation alone. Those inside the community are impacted both individually and societally by this representation. The accessibility and accuracy of such media is imperative. Media representation is a deceivingly complex issue, as it expects all minority experience to be represented through often singular narratives. Our group decided to look at queer representation in media to further understand the importance, impact, and misteps of this advocacy.

tristIan- Trans bodies represented in the world of art

Classical artwork that includes a figure only show a very narrow definition of beauty. In the world of classical art, beauty is confined to those who are strictly skinny, white, and “traditionally beautiful”. This definition does not include trans people. Challenging this archetype, I decided to paint a close friend of mine in a binder. Simply creating artwork that celebrates trans beauty is radical. I hope the viewer may look at this painting and recognise that a trans story is not only about oppression, but there is beauty, and honor. Trans bodies deserve to be hung up right next to Klimt’s ladies.

Good LGBT Representation is Boring

Media often waters down their LGBTQ+ characters in order to make them palatable to a cishet audience. Or shows that actually have good representation are not popular ones. Representation is good, and it does make a difference. Minority, and in the case: queer, representation in media has been shown to increase empathy and understanding towards that minority. However, in order for an audience to empathize with a character, that character has to be written well. Queer characters also have to be written well for a queer audience to identify with them.

Gazing at “It”: An Intersectional Analysis of Transnormativity and Black Womanhood in Orange is the New Black

Click here to open this PDF in a new window


Orange Is the new black Is a television show that showcases the experience of Queerness indeide the of the prison system. This show has the distinct honor of highlighting the experience of a trans black woman inside the prison system. Although this representation is revolutionary to the community, It is important to dissect what is going on critically. Sophia (a trans woman) befriends sister Ingalls in order to continue access to hormones behind bars. Sophia expresses her fear in loosing access to this life-saving care, saying "I’m not giving it up...I'm finally the woman God intended". This indicates that both her gender presentation and gender identity are equally important. Sister Ingalls chastises Sophia's desire to maintain her womanly figure and instead attempts to save her soul by making her a true woman. This rhetoric bypasses the importance of HRT (hormone replacement therapy) for trans people. By spreading the idea that being trans can be settled by self-love undermines a very integral experience of dysphoria to trans people.
Dysphoria can only be settled by medical access. Sophia has no sexual relationship or stated desire throughout the series. Sophia is desexualized, so that she may bypasses tropes of transgender women as sexual predators. Sophia’s personhood is modeled after Sister Ingalls, Disciplining Sophia into a desexualized, transgender subject within White, hegemonic femininity. The erasure of Sophias sexual needs also undermines the possibility of representation in media. Sexuality is looked at underneath a binary, and trans people do not fit under that, so how else is that dealt with other than completely written out? “Sister Ingalls represents the moral good of White women and their acceptance of trans women. Sophia's friendship with Sister Ingalls serves as evidence of her decency. Sister Ingalls gains a "daughter" and an audience on which to bestow her neoliberal, religious wisdom.” Narratives on trans issues are loaded when they revolve around the oppressor offering salvation. Historically, Christian belief Is that of the oppressor on queer issues. To depict Sister Ingalls as the savior asks the viewer to forget of the years of oppression Sophia has faced underneath a Christian Society. Sophia is depicted as morally questionable, while the oppressor offers clear, godly guidance. It is simply not fair to ask the oppressor and oppressed to link arms and waltz into a future of equality. Offering narratives like this in media shows the success of bridging cultural oppression, and does not show the extent of the pain behind these relationships. The viewer may go forward expecting tolerance of the oppressed.

Sex Stereotyping towards Black Gay Man in Moonlight Movie: A Queer Study

Click here to open this PDF in a new window


Moonlight, focuses on a gay black man named Chiron who is growing up in Liberty City, Florida. The movie is about stages in Chirons life, and how he takes romantic interest, and yet at the same time it focuses on many stereotypes as well. Chirons childhood friend, Kevin is one of the main interests, and this is very interesting because of how he is described. Kevin is shown as a typical hypermasculine, “player”, and it is a major plot twist when him and Chiron share a sexual experience. The movie overall represents the queer community, and the black community as well. This movie is now a part of cinema history, as a film featuring gay love would be something that would’ve been impossible a decade or two ago.
